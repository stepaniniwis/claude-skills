# Paragraph Structure Guide

Based on the PEEL framework (University of Hull) and the Rao argument model.

## The Academic Paragraph

A paragraph is the fundamental unit of academic argument. Each paragraph makes one claim and supports it. When you start a new claim, start a new paragraph.

**Target length**: 200-300 words. Shorter paragraphs lack development. Longer paragraphs usually contain multiple ideas and should be split.

## PEEL Structure

### P - Point (Topic Sentence)

The first sentence states the paragraph's claim. This is not a preview—it's the argument itself.

**Weak**: "This section discusses trust."  
**Strong**: "Trust functions not as interpersonal sentiment but as institutional infrastructure."

The topic sentence should be specific enough that a reader could predict what evidence follows.

**Test**: List only your topic sentences in order. Do they form a coherent argument outline? If not, restructure.

### E - Evidence

Evidence supports the point. In academic writing, evidence typically means:

1. **Cited sources**: Scholarly literature supporting the claim
2. **Data**: Empirical findings (in empirical papers)
3. **Examples**: Concrete illustrations of abstract claims
4. **Logical reasoning**: Deductive arguments from established premises

Evidence must be integrated, not listed. Each piece connects to the point.

**Listed (weak)**:
"Smith (2020) found X. Jones (2019) found Y. Brown (2021) found Z."

**Integrated (strong)**:
"This dynamic manifests across institutional contexts: in electoral systems (Smith, 2020), judicial processes (Jones, 2019), and administrative agencies (Brown, 2021)."

### E - Explanation (Analysis)

This is where you answer "So what?" Evidence alone proves nothing—you must interpret it.

**Explanation does three things**:
1. Connects evidence to the point
2. Draws out implications
3. Advances the larger argument

**Without explanation**:
"Studies show trust correlates with legitimacy (Smith, 2020)."

**With explanation**:
"Studies show trust correlates with legitimacy (Smith, 2020), suggesting that legitimacy is not merely a property of institutions but an achievement requiring ongoing cultivation of citizen confidence."

The explanation is your voice, your analysis. It requires no citation—it's your contribution.

### L - Link (Transition)

The link connects to the next paragraph or back to the thesis. It can appear at the end of a paragraph or the beginning of the next (sometimes split between both).

**Types of links**:
- **Forward**: "This raises the question of..."
- **Backward**: "Building on this foundation..."
- **Contrastive**: "However, this mechanism operates differently when..."
- **Thesis-connecting**: "This dynamic illustrates the paper's central claim that..."

## Complete Paragraph Example

**Point**: Electoral legitimacy requires more than procedural correctness—it demands that citizens experience the process as trustworthy.

**Evidence**: Empirical studies consistently find that procedural compliance alone fails to secure legitimacy; voters who doubt electoral integrity withhold consent regardless of legal regularity (Norris, 2014; Birch, 2010). This pattern intensifies in contexts of technological mediation, where verification mechanisms remain opaque to ordinary citizens (Alvarez et al., 2013).

**Explanation**: The disconnect between legal validity and perceived legitimacy suggests that trust operates through experiential rather than juridical channels. Citizens judge elections not by whether correct procedures were followed, but by whether they themselves can verify correctness. When verification exceeds ordinary competence—as with cryptographic voting systems—legitimacy cannot rest on transparency alone; it requires new infrastructures of trust.

**Link**: This experiential dimension of legitimacy poses particular challenges for digital voting systems, which must reconcile technical verifiability with citizen comprehension.

## Common Paragraph Problems

### Problem 1: Missing Point

**Symptom**: Paragraph begins with background or evidence, not a claim.

**Example**: "Many scholars have studied electoral integrity. Smith (2020) examined..."

**Fix**: What does this paragraph argue? Lead with that.

### Problem 2: Missing Explanation

**Symptom**: Evidence followed immediately by new evidence or new point.

**Example**: "Trust correlates with legitimacy (Smith, 2020). Jones (2019) found similar results. These findings matter for..."

**Fix**: After evidence, explain what it means before moving on.

### Problem 3: Too Many Points

**Symptom**: Paragraph contains multiple claims, often signaled by mid-paragraph topic shifts.

**Test**: Can you summarize the paragraph in one sentence? If you need "and" between two different claims, split the paragraph.

### Problem 4: No Link

**Symptom**: Paragraphs feel disconnected; reader loses the argument thread.

**Fix**: End with implication that sets up next paragraph, or begin next paragraph with explicit connection.

### Problem 5: Wrong Length

**Too short (<150 words)**: Usually missing explanation or evidence. Develop more.

**Too long (>350 words)**: Usually contains multiple points. Split it.

## Paragraph Types in Theoretical Papers

### Literature Synthesis Paragraph

**Point**: States what the literature collectively establishes or fails to establish.

**Evidence**: Multiple sources organized around a theme, not listed sequentially.

**Explanation**: Your assessment of what this literature means, what it misses.

**Example point**: "Existing research on electoral trust treats procedural and substantive legitimacy as analytically distinct, obscuring their practical entanglement."

### Theory-Building Paragraph

**Point**: Your theoretical claim.

**Evidence**: Logical reasoning, perhaps supported by citations to foundational texts.

**Explanation**: Why this claim advances the field's understanding.

**Example point**: "Trust in electoral systems operates not as interpersonal affect but as infrastructure—a distributed verification network that transforms individual uncertainty into collective confidence."

### Application/Implication Paragraph

**Point**: What follows from your theoretical contribution.

**Evidence**: Concrete examples of how the theory applies.

**Explanation**: Why these applications matter.

**Example point**: "Reconceiving trust as infrastructure suggests new design principles for digital voting systems—principles centered on citizen verifiability rather than mere technical security."

## Transitions Between Paragraphs

### Additive
"Moreover..." / "Furthermore..." / "This pattern extends to..."

### Contrastive
"However..." / "In contrast..." / "Despite this..."

### Causal
"Consequently..." / "This explains why..." / "As a result..."

### Sequential
"First... Second... Third..." / "Having established X, we now turn to Y..."

### Intensifying
"More significantly..." / "Crucially..." / "This becomes especially apparent when..."

## Revision Strategy

1. **Read only topic sentences**: Do they form a logical argument?
2. **Check each paragraph**: Point, Evidence, Explanation, Link present?
3. **Measure length**: 200-300 words?
4. **Test transitions**: Does each paragraph connect to the next?
5. **Cut mercilessly**: Can any sentence be removed without loss?
