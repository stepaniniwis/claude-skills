# Academic Style Guide

Based on Helen Sword's *Stylish Academic Writing* and the 5 Cs framework.

## The Guiding Principle

**"Elegant ideas deserve elegant expression."**

Complexity of thought does not require complexity of prose. The most sophisticated arguments often find their clearest expression in direct, concrete language.

## Helen Sword's 6 Cs

### 1. Communication

Write to be understood. Every sentence should transmit meaning efficiently.

**Test**: Could an intelligent reader outside your subfield follow the argument?

**Anti-pattern**: "The implementation of participatory mechanisms facilitates the actualization of democratic legitimacy through citizen engagement modalities."

**Better**: "When citizens participate meaningfully in governance, they come to see political decisions as legitimate."

### 2. Craft

Writing is a skill, not a talent. It improves through deliberate practice and revision.

**Practice**: Read your sentences aloud. Awkwardness in speech signals awkwardness in prose.

**Revision rule**: Every draft should be shorter than the last. Cut until it hurts, then cut more.

### 3. Creativity

Conventions exist to serve clarity, not to constrain thought. Break rules when they obstruct understanding.

**Permission**: Use "I" when you mean yourself. Use short sentences for emphasis. Start paragraphs with questions.

**Limit**: Creativity serves the reader, not the writer's self-expression.

### 4. Concrete Language

Abstract nouns drain vitality. Concrete nouns and active verbs energize prose.

**Abstract**: "The examination of the situation revealed the necessity for implementation."

**Concrete**: "We examined the voting records and found that fraud detection required new protocols."

### 5. Choice

Every word is a decision. Passive constructions, hedges, and nominalizations are choices—often poor ones made unconsciously.

**Exercise**: For each sentence, ask: "Did I choose this construction, or did it happen to me?"

### 6. Courage

Intellectual audacity distinguishes memorable scholarship. Take positions. Make claims. Defend them.

**Weak**: "It could perhaps be argued that trust may play some role in legitimacy."

**Courageous**: "Trust is not merely one factor among many—it is the precondition for democratic legitimacy."

## The 5 Cs of Academic Excellence

### 1. Clarity

One idea per sentence. One theme per paragraph. One argument per paper.

**Diagnostic**: Can you summarize each paragraph in one sentence? If not, it contains too many ideas or none at all.

**Sentence-level clarity**:
- Subject near the beginning
- Verb follows promptly
- Modifiers adjacent to what they modify

### 2. Coherence

Every paragraph connects to the one before and after. Every section advances the thesis.

**Coherence devices**:
- Transition words: "however," "therefore," "moreover," "in contrast"
- Structural signposting: "Three factors explain this phenomenon..."
- Backward reference: "This tension between..."

**Test**: Remove all topic sentences. Does the argument still flow? If yes, your transitions work.

### 3. Competence

Demonstrate mastery of relevant literature without listing it.

**Competence shows through**:
- Precise use of technical terms
- Awareness of debates and their stakes
- Selective citation (the right sources, not all sources)
- Confidence in your own contribution

**Incompetence shows through**:
- Hedge words everywhere
- Over-citation (insecurity)
- Under-citation (ignorance)
- Misuse of key terms

### 4. Comprehensive

Address counterarguments, limitations, and alternative explanations.

**Not comprehensive**: "This framework explains democratic legitimacy."

**Comprehensive**: "This framework explains one mechanism of democratic legitimacy—procedural trust—while leaving aside questions of distributive justice and historical contingency that other approaches foreground."

Acknowledging limits strengthens credibility.

### 5. Critique

Evaluate sources and arguments, including your own. Scholarship advances through critical engagement, not mere description.

**Descriptive**: "Habermas argues that communicative action underlies democracy."

**Critical**: "Habermas's account of communicative action provides powerful normative resources but struggles to accommodate strategic dimensions of actual political discourse—a gap this paper addresses through..."

## Anti-Patterns and Fixes

### Zombie Nouns (Nominalizations)

Verbs transformed into lifeless nouns.

| Zombie | Living |
|--------|--------|
| implementation | implement |
| utilization | use |
| facilitation | help, enable |
| conceptualization | conceive, think about |
| operationalization | measure, apply |
| examination | examine |
| determination | determine |
| transformation | transform |

**Zombie**: "The implementation of the framework led to the transformation of governance."

**Living**: "Implementing the framework transformed governance."

### Hedging Stacks

Piling qualifications that evacuate meaning.

**Over-hedged**: "It might perhaps be tentatively suggested that there could possibly be some evidence..."

**Appropriately hedged**: "The evidence suggests..."

**Use hedges when**: Genuinely uncertain, preliminary findings, contested territory.

**Avoid hedges when**: Making central claims, reporting established facts, stating your argument.

### Passive Voice (Misused)

Passive voice hides agency. Sometimes appropriate; often evasive.

**Evasive passive**: "Mistakes were made." (By whom?)

**Appropriate passive**: "The samples were analyzed using spectroscopy." (Agent irrelevant)

**Test**: If you could add "by zombies" and the sentence still makes sense, reconsider the passive.

### Noun Strings

Multiple nouns stacked without prepositions.

**Stacked**: "community trust development implementation strategy framework"

**Unstacked**: "a framework for strategies that implement approaches to developing community trust"

Still awkward—better to rethink entirely:

**Clear**: "strategies for building trust in communities"

### Throat-Clearing

Opening sentences that delay the point.

**Throat-clearing**: "In today's increasingly complex and interconnected world, it is important to consider the many ways in which..."

**Direct**: "Three mechanisms connect electoral integrity to democratic legitimacy."

## Sentence Variety

### Length

Mix sentence lengths. Short sentences emphasize. Long sentences develop complexity. Very short sentences punch.

### Structure

Not every sentence needs subject-verb-object order. Occasional inversion, questions, or fragments (used deliberately) create rhythm.

**Monotonous**: "Trust matters. Legitimacy requires trust. Citizens need to trust institutions. Institutions must earn trust."

**Varied**: "Trust matters—not as one factor among many, but as the precondition for legitimacy. Without it, institutions may command compliance but never consent. And consent, not compliance, distinguishes democratic governance from mere administration."

## Opening Sentences

The first sentence of each paragraph carries disproportionate weight. It should:

1. State the paragraph's main claim
2. Connect to the previous paragraph (if not the first)
3. Intrigue the reader to continue

**Weak opener**: "There are many factors to consider."

**Strong opener**: "Electoral legitimacy rests on a paradox: the same mechanisms that secure votes may undermine trust in their security."

## Revision Checklist

For each paragraph:
- [ ] Does the first sentence state the point?
- [ ] Are zombie nouns eliminated?
- [ ] Is passive voice justified where used?
- [ ] Does every sentence advance the argument?
- [ ] Can any words be cut?

For the whole paper:
- [ ] Is the thesis clear and courageous?
- [ ] Does every section serve the thesis?
- [ ] Are counterarguments addressed?
- [ ] Would an outsider understand it?
