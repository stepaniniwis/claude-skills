# Citation Integration Guide (APA 7th Edition)

## Fundamental Principle

**The argument leads. The source supports.**

Academic writing presents ideas, not bibliographies. Citations validate claims; they don't structure prose.

## Citation Placement Patterns

### Pattern 1: Claim-First (Default)

```
Democratic legitimacy requires more than procedural compliance; it demands citizens' substantive engagement with governance processes (Habermas, 1996; Dryzek, 2000).
```

The reader encounters the idea fully before learning its scholarly pedigree.

### Pattern 2: Source Integration (When Author Matters)

Use only when the source's identity is analytically relevant:

```
Luhmann's systems-theoretic approach reframes trust not as interpersonal affect but as a mechanism for complexity reduction (Luhmann, 1979), a distinction that proves critical for understanding digital voting systems.
```

Here, attributing the idea to Luhmann matters because you're invoking a specific theoretical tradition.

### Pattern 3: Multiple Source Synthesis

```
Three distinct mechanisms connect electoral integrity to democratic legitimacy: procedural fairness, outcome acceptance, and institutional trust (Norris, 2014; Birch, 2010; Schedler, 2002).
```

Synthesize first, cite all contributing sources together.

## When NOT to Lead with Author

**Avoid**: "According to Smith (2020), trust is important."  
**Avoid**: "Jones et al. (2019) found that voters prefer transparency."  
**Avoid**: "As Brown (2021) argues, legitimacy requires participation."

These constructions:
- Waste words on attribution rather than argument
- Fragment the reader's attention
- Suggest the writer is reporting rather than analyzing

## When to Quote Directly

Direct quotation is appropriate when:

1. **Definitions**: "Habermas defines communicative action as 'action oriented to reaching understanding' (1984, p. 86)."

2. **Contested terms**: When the exact phrasing is itself under debate.

3. **Particularly eloquent formulations**: Rare—most ideas can be better paraphrased.

4. **Analyzing language**: When the word choice itself is your object of analysis.

**Rule of thumb**: If you can paraphrase without losing meaning, paraphrase.

## Paraphrasing Technique

### Poor Paraphrase (Too Close to Original)
Original: "Trust operates as a mechanism for reducing social complexity."  
Poor: "Trust functions as a mechanism for reducing complexity in society."

### Strong Paraphrase (Genuine Reformulation)
Strong: "Complex social systems rely on trust to filter the overwhelming range of possible interactions into manageable expectations."

The strong paraphrase demonstrates understanding, not just synonym substitution.

## Citation Frequency

### Over-Citation Signals
- Every sentence has a citation → You're reporting, not arguing
- Same source cited 5+ times in one paragraph → Over-reliance on single source
- Citations after obvious claims → Insecurity about your analysis

### Under-Citation Signals
- Specific claims without sources → Unsubstantiated assertions
- "Research shows..." without citation → Vague appeal to authority
- Entire paragraphs without sources → Either your analysis (fine) or missing support (problem)

## APA 7th Quick Reference

### In-Text Citations

| Type | First Citation | Subsequent |
|------|---------------|------------|
| 1-2 authors | (Smith & Jones, 2023) | (Smith & Jones, 2023) |
| 3+ authors | (Smith et al., 2023) | (Smith et al., 2023) |
| Organization | (World Health Organization, 2023) | (WHO, 2023) |
| No date | (Smith, n.d.) | (Smith, n.d.) |
| Page number | (Smith, 2023, p. 45) | For direct quotes |

### Multiple Sources

Alphabetical by first author, separated by semicolons:
```
(Adams, 2020; Brown, 2019; Chen et al., 2021)
```

### Secondary Sources (Cite Sparingly)

When citing a source you found within another source:
```
Luhmann's concept of trust (as cited in Möllering, 2006) provides...
```

Only use when the original is truly inaccessible.

## Common Errors

| Error | Problem | Fix |
|-------|---------|-----|
| "Smith states that..." | Author-led, passive | Lead with claim |
| (Smith, 2020, Jones, 2019) | Missing semicolon | (Smith, 2020; Jones, 2019) |
| "The study found..." | Which study? | Name or cite |
| Citing after every clause | Over-citation | Group at sentence end |
| "Some researchers argue..." | Vague | Name them or cite |

## Integration Examples

### Literature Review Paragraph (Good)

"Electoral integrity encompasses three distinct dimensions: the quality of electoral laws, the impartiality of electoral administration, and citizens' perceptions of fairness (Norris, 2014). While legal and administrative dimensions receive substantial scholarly attention, perceptual legitimacy remains undertheorized, particularly in contexts where procedural compliance coexists with public distrust (Birch, 2010). This gap proves especially consequential for digital voting systems, where technical verification mechanisms may satisfy legal requirements while failing to generate citizen confidence (Alvarez et al., 2013). The disconnect suggests that legitimacy in electoral contexts operates through mechanisms distinct from mere procedural correctness."

**Note**: Four sources integrated into a coherent argument. Each citation supports a specific claim. The final sentence is the author's analysis—no citation needed.

### Literature Review Paragraph (Poor)

"Smith (2020) discusses electoral integrity. According to Jones (2019), legitimacy is important. Brown (2021) argues that trust matters. Chen et al. (2022) found that voters care about fairness. This shows that electoral integrity is a complex topic."

**Problems**: Author-led citations, no synthesis, no argument, conclusion states the obvious.
