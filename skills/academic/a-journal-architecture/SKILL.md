# A-Journal Paper Architecture

What award-winning A-journal papers have in common — not style, but intellectual structure. Use this skill to diagnose whether a paper has the architecture that survives review at JMS, AMJ, ASQ, OS, SMJ, or Strategy Science.

## Origin

Derived from analysis of six JMS award/exemplar papers: Building Character (longitudinal hybrid identity), Causal Triangulation (causal inference methodology), Serendipity (systematic review + conceptualization), Stakeholder Governance (responsible innovation), How Are Skills Changing with Digital Technologies (systematic review), The Acceleration of AI (conceptual). Pattern holds across empirical, conceptual, and review papers.

## The Seven Tests

Run these sequentially on any paper targeting an A-journal. Each test produces a verdict: PASS, WEAK, or FAIL.

### Test 1: Big Puzzle, Not Small Gap

The paper must open with a problem that many researchers recognize as consequential — not a narrow niche, not "nobody has studied X in context Y."

**Pass:** The opening problem touches multiple research conversations and makes readers think "yes, that matters."
**Fail:** The opening reads as gap-spotting ("surprisingly little research has examined...").

**Diagnostic question:** If a researcher in an adjacent field read only the first paragraph, would they recognize the problem as important to THEIR work?

Award papers open with: hybrid identity under organizational pressure, weak causal claims across management research, conceptual confusion around serendipity, governance failures in responsible innovation, skill transformation under digitalization, theoretical drift caused by treating AI as monolith.

### Test 2: Intellectual Deficiency, Not Missing Context

The gap must be framed as a deficiency in how the field THINKS, not as a missing empirical context.

**Pass:** "Existing accounts assume X, but this assumption obscures Y" or "The field conflates A and B, which prevents understanding of C."
**Fail:** "No study has examined this phenomenon in [country/industry/population]."

**Diagnostic question:** Does the gap statement identify a flaw in theory, concepts, or analytical approach — or just a missing data point?

### Test 3: Named Theoretical Device

The paper must produce a transportable intellectual artifact: a named mechanism, a model, a typology, a set of necessary conditions, a process framework. Something a reviewer can paraphrase in one sentence and that other researchers can apply elsewhere.

**Pass:** After reading, a reviewer can say "This paper introduces [device] which explains [mechanism]."
**Fail:** The paper produces findings but no portable conceptual contribution.

**Examples from award papers:** re-keying (mechanism), causal triangulation (model), three necessary conditions of serendipity (boundary definition), three-stage stakeholder governance model, four skill framings, four AI types.

**Diagnostic question:** What is the one thing a doctoral student would cite this paper FOR five years from now? Can you name it in three words?

### Test 4: Paper-in-One-Sentence

There must be a single sentence — ideally in the first third of the Introduction — that a reviewer would paraphrase in their review. This sentence captures the entire argument: puzzle + mechanism + outcome.

**Pass:** The sentence exists and is memorable.
**Fail:** The argument is spread across paragraphs but never crystallized.

**Template:** "This paper explains why [puzzle] by showing that [mechanism], which produces [outcome]."

**Test:** Read the Introduction. Can you find the one sentence? If not, write it. If you cannot write it, the paper does not yet have a clear enough argument.

### Test 5: Contribution Hierarchy

Multiple contributions must be ranked, not listed as equals. The strongest contribution leads. Weaker contributions (especially methodological) are subordinated or dropped.

**Pass:** The primary contribution is unmistakably identified and given the most space. Secondary contributions are flagged as such.
**Fail:** Three contributions listed on equal footing, giving the impression of trying to win on too many fronts.

**Diagnostic question:** If a reviewer forced you to keep only ONE contribution, which would it be? That one leads. The others serve it.

### Test 6: Concept Boundary Defense

The central concept must be defended against adjacent concepts. Reviewers will always ask: "Is this not just [existing concept] under a new label?"

**Pass:** The paper contains explicit contrast sentences: "X is not Y because..." for at least three neighboring concepts.
**Fail:** The new concept is defined but never differentiated from what already exists.

**Template for boundary defense:**
"[New concept] is not simply [adjacent concept A], because [specific distinction]. It is not equivalent to [adjacent concept B], because [specific distinction]. And it is not reducible to [adjacent concept C], because [specific distinction]."

These three sentences must appear immediately after the first definition of the new concept.

### Test 7: Portability Beyond Setting

The paper must show that its theoretical device travels beyond the specific empirical setting. The setting is the revealing case, not the scope limit.

**Pass:** The Discussion contains a concrete paragraph showing how the mechanism applies to at least two other contexts.
**Fail:** The paper reads as a case study of one setting with a theoretical label attached.

**Diagnostic formula:** "[Setting] is the revealing case, not the terminal scope condition. The mechanism should travel to any [general class of fields/organizations] in which [abstract version of the conditions]."

After running the seven tests, verify:

| Element | Location | Status |

## Mechanism-Structure-Outcome Naming

The strongest papers have three named elements in a chain:

- **Mechanism** (the process): what happens and why. E.g., convergent neglect, re-keying, causal triangulation
- **Structural location** (where it happens): the institutional/organizational space. E.g., governance interstitium, hybrid identity space, knowledge boundary
- **Outcome** (what results): the observable consequence. E.g., selective adaptation, identity coherence, robust inference

All three should be named, defined, and linked. The Abstract should contain all three. The Theory Section should derive them. The Findings should demonstrate them.

**Diagnostic question:** Can you fill in: "[Mechanism] produces [Outcome] because [Structural Location] exists"? If not, the chain is incomplete.

## Revision Priorities (When Diagnosing an Existing Draft)

If the paper fails multiple tests, prioritize fixes in this order:

1. Paper-in-one-sentence (Test 4) — without this, nothing else holds
2. Named theoretical device (Test 3) — without this, no contribution
3. Concept boundary defense (Test 6) — without this, reviewers reject as relabeling
4. Contribution hierarchy (Test 5) — without this, the paper feels scattered
5. Big puzzle (Test 1) — can usually be fixed by reframing the opening
6. Portability (Test 7) — usually needs only one Discussion paragraph
7. Intellectual deficiency (Test 2) — usually fixable by rewriting the gap statement

## Anti-Patterns

| Anti-pattern | What it looks like | Fix |
|---|---|---|
| Gap-spotting | "No study has examined X in Y" | Reframe as intellectual deficiency |
| Contribution inflation | Three equally weighted contributions | Hierarchize: one leads, others serve |
| Setting trap | Paper reads as defense/healthcare/fintech case study | Add portability paragraph + abstract version of conditions |
| Label-without-mechanism | New term introduced but no process explained | Define mechanism + structural location + outcome |
| Defensive breadth | Paper covers everything to preempt all criticism | Cut: better to be deep on one front than shallow on three |
| Missing contrast sentences | New concept defined but not differentiated | Add three "X is not Y because..." sentences |
| Buried insight | The best sentence is on page 14 of Discussion | Move it to Introduction, first third |
