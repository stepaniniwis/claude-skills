## 1. Mediation Framework

**Structure**: X → M → Y (Independent variable affects outcome through a mediating mechanism)

**When to use**: When you're not just claiming that X affects Y, but explaining WHY — specifying the causal pathway.

**Theory section logic**:
1. Establish the X → Y relationship (direct effect, grounded in prior work)
2. Introduce M as the mechanism (why does X lead to Y?)
3. Argue for X → M (why does X produce the mediating state?)
4. Argue for M → Y (why does the mediating state lead to the outcome?)
5. Argue that M accounts for (part of) the X → Y relationship

**Hypothesis structure**:
- H1: X is positively related to Y
- H2: M mediates the relationship between X and Y
- Or: H1: X is positively related to M; H2: M is positively related to Y; H3: M mediates the X-Y relationship

**Quality markers**:
- The mediator is theoretically distinct from both X and Y
- The mechanism is named and explained, not just statistically implied
- Temporal ordering is plausible (X precedes M precedes Y)

## 2. Moderation Framework

**Structure**: The X → Y relationship depends on W (moderator)

**When to use**: When existing findings are contradictory and you suspect context matters, or when you're specifying boundary conditions.

**Theory section logic**:
1. Establish the X → Y relationship
2. Introduce contradictory findings or theoretical reasons for variation
3. Introduce W as the contextual factor that explains the variation
4. Argue for the interaction: why does W change the X → Y relationship?
5. Specify direction: does W strengthen, weaken, or reverse the relationship?

**Hypothesis structure**:
- H1: W moderates the relationship between X and Y, such that the relationship is [stronger/weaker/reversed] when W is [high/present]

**Quality markers**:
- Moderation is theoretically motivated, not just data-driven
- The interaction mechanism is specified (not just "W matters")
- You explain why W changes the relationship, not just that it does

## 3. Moderated Mediation / Mediated Moderation

**Structure**: The mediated pathway (X → M → Y) varies across levels of a moderator W; OR the moderation effect (W on X→Y) operates through a mediator.

**When to use**: When the story is more complex than simple mediation or moderation. Use sparingly — complexity must be theoretically justified.

**Theory section logic**:
1. Build the mediation story first (X → M → Y)
2. Introduce the moderator and argue where it intervenes in the pathway
3. Specify which path is moderated: first stage (X → M), second stage (M → Y), or both
4. Provide distinct theoretical arguments for each moderated path

**Quality markers**:
- The moderation point is theoretically specified, not all paths moderated for good measure
- Each component relationship has its own theoretical argument
- The overall model can be explained in one coherent narrative paragraph

## 4. Process Framework

**Structure**: Phenomenon unfolds in stages or phases, with distinct mechanisms operating at each stage.

**When to use**: When the phenomenon is inherently dynamic — things change over time, and the same variables play different roles at different stages.

**Theory section logic**:
1. Identify the process phases and justify why these (not other) phases
2. For each phase: specify the dominant mechanism, key constructs, and transition trigger
3. Explain how phases connect — what triggers movement from one to the next?
4. Address recursion or feedback loops if present

**Proposition structure** (propositions more common than hypotheses for process models):
- P1: In the [initiation] phase, [mechanism A] drives [outcome]
- P2: The transition from [initiation] to [development] is triggered by [event/threshold]
- P3: In the [development] phase, [mechanism B] replaces [mechanism A] as the dominant driver

**Quality markers**:
- Phases are theoretically distinct, not arbitrary time slices
- Transition mechanisms are specified
- The process adds explanatory value beyond a static model

## 5. Typology / Taxonomy

**Structure**: The phenomenon comes in distinct types, each with different characteristics and dynamics.

**When to use**: When lumping together what should be separated obscures important variation. Typologies create order from heterogeneity.

**Theory section logic**:
1. Identify the dimensions that differentiate types (and justify why these dimensions)
2. Cross the dimensions to generate types
3. Define each type with clear boundaries
4. Explain what differs across types (different mechanisms, outcomes, or boundary conditions)
5. Argue for theoretical and empirical distinctiveness

**Quality markers**:
- Dimensions are theoretically grounded, not inductively convenient
- Types are mutually exclusive and collectively exhaustive (or justification for why not)
- The typology generates non-obvious predictions (types that behave differently in unexpected ways)
- Parsimony: 2×2 or 2×3 preferred; anything larger needs strong justification

## 6. Multi-Level Framework

**Structure**: Constructs and mechanisms operate across levels of analysis (individual, team, organization, field, etc.)

**When to use**: When the phenomenon inherently spans levels — e.g., individual decisions that produce organizational outcomes, or institutional pressures that shape individual behavior.

**Theory section logic**:
1. Specify the levels involved and justify why multiple levels are necessary
2. For each level: define constructs at that level
3. Specify cross-level mechanisms explicitly:
   - Top-down: How higher-level factors constrain/enable lower-level processes
   - Bottom-up: How lower-level processes aggregate/emerge to higher-level patterns
   - Cross-level interaction: How variables at different levels interact
4. Address the aggregation problem: how do individual-level phenomena become collective?

**Quality markers**:
- Each construct is assigned a clear level of analysis
- Cross-level mechanisms are named and justified, not just assumed
- Aggregation/emergence mechanisms are specified
- The multi-level structure is necessary (single-level model would miss something important)

## 7. Paradox Framework

**Structure**: The phenomenon involves inherent contradictions or tensions that cannot be resolved but must be managed.

**When to use**: When binary either/or thinking fails to capture the phenomenon — when both sides of a contradiction are simultaneously true and necessary.

**Theory section logic**:
1. Identify the paradox: what two things are simultaneously true and contradictory?
2. Show why the contradiction is inherent (not resolvable by choosing one side)
3. Theorize how actors navigate the paradox (coping strategies, oscillation, transcendence)
4. Specify outcomes of different navigation approaches

**Quality markers**:
- The paradox is genuine (not just a tradeoff or dilemma)
- Both poles are theoretically developed, not just the "interesting" one
- Navigation mechanisms are specified
- The framework explains variation in outcomes through variation in navigation

## 8. Selecting the Right Archetype

| Your Theoretical Question | Best Archetype |
|--------------------------|----------------|
| "Why does X lead to Y?" | Mediation |
| "When does X lead to Y?" | Moderation |
| "How does the X→Y process unfold over time?" | Process |
| "Are there fundamentally different types of this phenomenon?" | Typology |
| "How do individual-level and org-level factors interact?" | Multi-level |
| "Why do actors face irreconcilable demands?" | Paradox |
| "What's the full story of how, when, and why?" | Moderated Mediation |

**Combinations are common and valid.** Many strong papers combine archetypes — e.g., a multi-level mediation model, or a process framework with typological variation. The key is that each layer of complexity is theoretically justified, not added for sophistication signaling.

**Parsimony rule**: Start with the simplest archetype that captures your theoretical argument. Add complexity only when the simpler model demonstrably fails to explain what you observe.
