# Tension Resolution Patterns

Extended guidance on surfacing and resolving theoretical tensions — the core move that transforms a literature synthesis into a theoretical contribution.

## 1. Types of Theoretical Tension

### Definitional Tension
Same term, different meanings across literature streams. Often the most insidious because it masquerades as agreement.

**Signal**: Two papers seem to agree on a construct but measure it differently, predict different outcomes, or apply it at different levels.

**Example**: "Trust" in institutional contexts (calculative, system-level) vs. interpersonal contexts (affective, dyadic). Papers citing both without distinguishing the type produce incoherent frameworks.

**Resolution move**: Disambiguate explicitly. "We distinguish between institutional trust (defined as...) and interpersonal trust (defined as...). Our framework focuses on [one/both] because [reason]."

### Level-of-Analysis Tension
Theory developed at one level applied at another without cross-level justification.

**Signal**: Individual-level psychological mechanisms invoked to explain organizational outcomes, or vice versa.

**Example**: Individual technology acceptance (TAM) applied to organizational adoption without specifying the aggregation mechanism.

**Resolution move**: Specify the cross-level mechanism. "Individual acceptance aggregates to organizational adoption through [champion effects / critical mass dynamics / mandate compliance], depending on [governance structure]."

### Contextual Tension
Theory holds in context A but not context B, and both contexts are relevant to the phenomenon.

**Signal**: Studies showing contradictory findings in different settings without explanation.

**Example**: Digital transformation increases efficiency in private sector but shows mixed results in public sector.

**Resolution move**: Identify the moderating condition. "We propose that [contextual factor] moderates this relationship: in contexts characterized by [A], the relationship is [positive], while in contexts characterized by [B], it [attenuates/reverses] because [mechanism]."

### Paradigmatic Tension
Fundamental epistemological or ontological disagreements between theoretical traditions.

**Signal**: Theories that cannot be combined because they assume different things about reality (e.g., rational choice vs. institutional logics).

**Resolution move**: Strategic bracketing or meta-theoretical integration. Either scope to one paradigm with explicit justification, or argue for a pragmatic integration that preserves the useful insights from each while acknowledging their different philosophical commitments.

### Temporal Tension
Theory assumes static relationships, but the phenomenon is dynamic.

**Signal**: Cross-sectional theories applied to processes; stage models that don't specify transition mechanisms.

**Resolution move**: Temporalize the framework. Specify initial conditions, transition triggers, and how construct relationships change over time.

## 2. Diagnosis Framework

When encountering potential tension, work through these questions:

```
1. DETECT: Do two or more sources make incompatible claims about [phenomenon]?
   → If no: no tension (but check for hidden assumptions)
   → If yes: proceed to diagnose

2. CLASSIFY: What type of tension is this?
   → Same term, different meaning? → Definitional
   → Different levels of analysis? → Level-based
   → Different settings/conditions? → Contextual
   → Different paradigmatic assumptions? → Paradigmatic
   → Static vs. dynamic assumptions? → Temporal

3. ASSESS STAKES: How consequential is this tension for your framework?
   → Central to your argument? → Must resolve
   → Adjacent but relevant? → Address explicitly, resolve or bracket
   → Peripheral? → Acknowledge in limitations

4. RESOLVE: Apply the appropriate strategy (see Section 3)
```

## 3. Resolution Strategies

### Integration
Show that competing perspectives are both correct under different conditions.

**Structure**: "Theory A predicts [X]. Theory B predicts [Y]. These predictions are not contradictory when we recognize that [moderating condition] determines which mechanism dominates. Specifically, [condition 1] activates [mechanism from A], while [condition 2] activates [mechanism from B]."

**Best for**: Contextual tensions, some level-of-analysis tensions.

### Synthesis
Create a higher-order framework that subsumes both perspectives.

**Structure**: "Both [Theory A] and [Theory B] capture aspects of [phenomenon], but neither accounts for [missing element]. We propose [new framework] that incorporates [A's insight about...] and [B's insight about...] while adding [new element] to explain [what neither alone could]."

**Best for**: Definitional tensions, paradigmatic tensions (when paradigms can be bridged).

### Dialectical Resolution
Thesis + antithesis → new synthesis that transcends both.

**Structure**: "The dominant view holds [thesis]. Critics argue [antithesis]. We propose that this debate itself reveals [deeper issue], and offer [synthesis] as a resolution that moves beyond the binary."

**Best for**: Entrenched debates where both sides have merit.

### Strategic Bracketing
Acknowledge the tension, explicitly scope your framework to one side, and justify why.

**Structure**: "We recognize the tension between [A] and [B]. For the purpose of this study, we adopt [A's] perspective because [justification]. This scoping decision means our framework [applies to / does not apply to] [contexts]."

**Best for**: Paradigmatic tensions that cannot be bridged, peripheral tensions.

## 4. Worked Examples

### Example 1: Technology Adoption Tensions

**Tension**: Rational models (TAM/UTAUT) predict adoption based on perceived usefulness and ease. Institutional models predict adoption based on mimetic, coercive, and normative pressures — regardless of individual perceptions.

**Diagnosis**: Paradigmatic (methodological individualism vs. structuralism) + Level-of-analysis (individual vs. organizational).

**Resolution (Integration)**: "Individual-level acceptance and institutional pressure operate simultaneously but through different mechanisms. We propose that institutional pressures set the decision context (whether adoption is voluntary, mandated, or socially expected), while individual perceptions determine engagement quality within that context. Thus, [institutional factor] moderates the relationship between [individual perception] and [adoption outcome]."

### Example 2: Identity and Entrepreneurship Tensions

**Tension**: Role identity theory treats identities as stable cognitive structures. Practice-based identity perspectives treat identity as emergent and processual.

**Diagnosis**: Paradigmatic (cognitivist vs. practice-based) + Temporal (static vs. dynamic).

**Resolution (Dialectical)**: "Rather than choosing between identity-as-structure and identity-as-process, we propose that hybrid entrepreneurs navigate this very tension in practice. Identity structures provide the cognitive resources for sensemaking, while identity processes enable adaptation to contradictory role demands. The dynamic interplay — not either alone — explains how hybrid entrepreneurs maintain coherence across incompatible institutional domains."

## 5. Anti-Patterns

### The False Synthesis
Claiming integration without actually resolving the tension. "We draw on both [Theory A] and [Theory B]" is not synthesis — it's juxtaposition.

**Test**: If you removed one theory, would the framework still work? If yes, you haven't integrated them.

### The Diplomatic Dodge
"Both perspectives have merit." This acknowledges tension without resolving it. Adequate for limitations sections, insufficient for theory development.

### The Cherry Pick
Using one aspect of a theory while ignoring its assumptions that contradict your framework. Theories are package deals — you can't take the constructs and leave the ontology without explicit justification.

### The Straw Man Resolution
Weakening one perspective to make your resolution seem more impressive. Represent competing perspectives at their strongest before showing why your resolution is needed.
