# Theoretical Contribution Types

Not all gaps are equal. "No one has studied X" is not a contribution—you must explain why studying X advances the field.

## The Contribution Question

Every paper must answer: **"What do we know after reading this that we didn't know before, and why does it matter?"**

Two parts:
1. **Novelty**: What's new?
2. **Significance**: Why does the new thing matter?

## Contribution Taxonomy

### 1. Theoretical Extension

**What you do**: Add concepts, mechanisms, or variables to an existing framework.

**Form**: "Theory X explains A and B. We show it also explains C, by adding mechanism M."

**Signal phrases**:
- "We extend X by incorporating..."
- "Building on X, we add..."
- "X can be enriched by attending to..."

**Example**: "Luhmann's trust theory focuses on interpersonal complexity reduction. We extend it to institutional contexts by introducing the concept of 'verification infrastructure' as a functional equivalent of interpersonal familiarity."

**Risk**: Extension without transformation—just adding variables doesn't advance theory.

### 2. Theoretical Synthesis

**What you do**: Integrate previously separate theoretical streams.

**Form**: "Theory X explains A. Theory Y explains B. We show X and Y are complementary, together explaining C."

**Signal phrases**:
- "Bringing together X and Y reveals..."
- "Integrating insights from X and Y..."
- "The intersection of X and Y illuminates..."

**Example**: "Deliberative democratic theory and systems-theoretic accounts of trust have developed in isolation. Integrating them reveals that democratic legitimacy requires both normative justification (deliberation) and complexity-reducing mechanisms (trust infrastructure)."

**Risk**: Superficial combination—real synthesis requires identifying deep complementarities or tensions.

### 3. Boundary Conditions

**What you do**: Identify where an established theory does/doesn't hold.

**Form**: "Theory X is believed to hold universally. We show it holds only under conditions C₁ and C₂."

**Signal phrases**:
- "X holds only when..."
- "The relationship between A and B is contingent on..."
- "Contrary to universal claims, X operates differently in context C..."

**Example**: "The positive relationship between transparency and trust, established in organizational contexts, reverses in high-complexity technical systems where transparency exposes information citizens cannot evaluate."

**Risk**: Trivial boundary conditions—finding that something works differently in obviously different contexts.

### 4. Conceptual Clarification

**What you do**: Resolve ambiguity, distinguish conflated concepts, or sharpen definitions.

**Form**: "Concept X is used ambiguously in the literature. We distinguish X₁ and X₂, showing they have different causes/effects."

**Signal phrases**:
- "We distinguish between..."
- "The concept of X conflates..."
- "Parsing X into components reveals..."

**Example**: "'Electoral integrity' conflates three distinct phenomena: legal compliance, administrative quality, and perceived legitimacy. These have different determinants and do not reliably co-occur."

**Risk**: Definitional hairsplitting—distinctions must have theoretical or empirical consequences.

### 5. Mechanism Identification

**What you do**: Explain *how* or *why* an established relationship holds.

**Form**: "It is known that X correlates with Y. We identify the mechanism M through which X produces Y."

**Signal phrases**:
- "The mechanism connecting X to Y..."
- "We explain how X produces Y..."
- "The process through which..."

**Example**: "Prior research establishes that E2E-V systems correlate with voter confidence. We identify the underlying mechanism: E2E-V transforms elections from trust-requiring to trust-producing events by enabling citizens to verify independently."

**Why valuable**: Mechanisms enable prediction, intervention, and generalization.

### 6. Problematization

**What you do**: Challenge fundamental assumptions underlying existing theory.

**Form**: "Theory X assumes A. We show A is problematic, requiring reconceptualization."

**Signal phrases**:
- "Contrary to received wisdom..."
- "The field has assumed... but..."
- "We challenge the premise that..."

**Example**: "Democratic theory assumes that transparency produces trust. We argue this relationship is theoretically contingent: transparency produces trust only when citizens can evaluate what is made transparent. Otherwise, transparency produces anxiety, not confidence."

**Risk**: Contrarianism without construction—problematization should open new avenues, not just critique.

### 7. New Phenomenon

**What you do**: Identify and name a previously unrecognized phenomenon.

**Form**: "We identify a phenomenon—which we term 'X'—that existing frameworks have not captured."

**Signal phrases**:
- "We introduce the concept of..."
- "We identify a phenomenon we call..."
- "A previously unrecognized dynamic..."

**Example**: "We identify 'verification paradox': the phenomenon whereby increasing the technical rigor of electoral verification can decrease rather than increase democratic legitimacy, by moving verification beyond citizen competence."

**Risk**: Neologism inflation—new terms need theoretical grounding and empirical purchase.

## Contribution Strength Criteria

### Novelty Gradient

| Level | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Incremental | Small extension of known ideas |
| Substantial | New mechanism, significant boundary condition |
| Fundamental | Reconceptualization, paradigm challenge |

### Significance Indicators

- **Scope**: How many phenomena does it explain?
- **Parsimony**: How efficiently does it explain them?
- **Fertility**: What new questions does it open?
- **Practical relevance**: Does it inform action?

## The "So What?" Gauntlet

Test your contribution against escalating challenges:

**Level 1**: "What's new?"
- Can you state in one sentence what readers learn?

**Level 2**: "Why does the new thing matter?"
- Does it change how we understand something?
- Does it resolve a puzzle or debate?
- Does it enable new research or practice?

**Level 3**: "Why does it matter *now*?"
- Is there urgency? A timely empirical context?
- Has something changed that makes this newly relevant?

**Level 4**: "Why does it matter *to this audience*?"
- Does it speak to the journal's concerns?
- Does it engage the field's active debates?

## Contribution Statement Templates

### Template 1: Extension
"While existing accounts of [TOPIC] emphasize [EXISTING FOCUS], they neglect [YOUR FOCUS]. This paper extends [FRAMEWORK] by introducing [NEW ELEMENT], showing that [FINDING/ARGUMENT]."

### Template 2: Synthesis
"[THEORY A] and [THEORY B] have developed largely in isolation. Bringing them together reveals [INSIGHT], which neither alone can provide."

### Template 3: Boundary Condition
"The established relationship between [X] and [Y] is typically assumed to hold universally. We show it is contingent on [CONDITION], reversing when [ALTERNATIVE CONDITION]."

### Template 4: Mechanism
"Prior research has established that [X] correlates with [Y], but the underlying mechanism remains unclear. We identify [MECHANISM] as the process connecting them."

### Template 5: Problematization
"Existing theory assumes [ASSUMPTION]. We show this assumption is problematic because [REASON], suggesting instead that [ALTERNATIVE]."

## Matching Gap to Contribution

| Gap Type | Natural Contribution |
|----------|---------------------|
| "X is understudied" | New phenomenon, extension |
| "X and Y not connected" | Synthesis, mechanism |
| "X overgeneralized" | Boundary conditions |
| "X conflates distinct things" | Conceptual clarification |
| "X assumes wrongly" | Problematization |
| "X correlates with Y but why?" | Mechanism |

## Common Weaknesses

| Weakness | Problem | Fix |
|----------|---------|-----|
| "Gap as contribution" | "No one studied X" isn't itself a contribution | Explain why studying X changes understanding |
| "Application as contribution" | "We apply X to new context" | Show what the application reveals about X |
| "Summary as contribution" | "We review literature on X" | Synthesize, don't just summarize |
| "Confirmation as contribution" | "We confirm X holds in context C" | Only contributes if C was theoretically uncertain |
