# CARS Model: Create A Research Space

John Swales' model for Introduction structure. The Introduction is not merely background—it's a strategic argument for why your paper deserves attention.

## The Logic of CARS

Academic publishing is a conversation. To join, you must:
1. Show you understand the ongoing discussion (Territory)
2. Identify something missing or wrong (Niche)
3. Offer to fix it (Occupation)

This is not optional. Papers lacking clear CARS moves get rejected as "no clear contribution."

## Move 1: Establishing Territory

**Goal**: Create shared ground with readers. Establish that your topic is part of an active, important scholarly conversation.

### Step 1a: Claiming Centrality

Assert the topic's importance to the field.

**Strong centrality claims**:
- "Democratic legitimacy remains one of the central puzzles of contemporary political theory."
- "The relationship between trust and institutions has attracted sustained scholarly attention across disciplines."
- "Few questions are more fundamental to electoral studies than how citizens come to accept election outcomes."

**Weak centrality claims** (avoid):
- "Many researchers have studied X." (So what?)
- "X is an interesting topic." (To whom?)
- "Recently, X has gained attention." (Why does that matter?)

**Test**: Does your centrality claim explain *why* the topic matters, not just *that* people study it?

### Step 1b: Making Topic Generalizations

Establish what is generally known or accepted.

**Examples**:
- "Electoral integrity typically encompasses three dimensions: legal frameworks, administrative implementation, and public perception."
- "Trust in institutions correlates strongly with democratic stability across regime types."

**Function**: Shows you command the literature and sets up the gap.

### Step 1c: Reviewing Previous Research

Survey relevant prior work—selectively, not exhaustively.

**Principles**:
- Organize thematically, not chronologically
- Cite to position, not to list
- Each citation should do argumentative work

**Example**:
"Procedural theories emphasize rule compliance (Smith, 2015; Jones, 2018), while substantive accounts foreground outcome quality (Brown, 2020). A third stream examines legitimacy as perception rather than property (Chen et al., 2019)."

This review positions three streams, setting up a gap about their integration.

## Move 2: Establishing Niche (The Critical Move)

**Goal**: Create the space your paper will fill. This is where most papers fail or succeed.

### What Makes a Good Gap?

| Gap Quality | Characteristic | Example |
|-------------|----------------|---------|
| **Consequential** | Filling it changes something | "This gap prevents understanding X" |
| **Tractable** | Can be addressed in one paper | Not "solve democracy" |
| **Novel** | Not already filled | Check recent literature |
| **Specific** | Clear boundaries | Not "more research needed" |

### Step 2a: Counter-Claiming

Challenge previous work directly.

**Examples**:
- "However, these accounts share a problematic assumption: that procedural correctness guarantees legitimacy."
- "Yet this framework cannot explain cases where legally valid elections fail to secure public acceptance."

**Caution**: Be fair. Acknowledge what prior work accomplishes before identifying limits.

### Step 2b: Indicating Gap

Identify what's missing.

**Strong gap indicators**:
- "What remains undertheorized is the mechanism connecting X to Y."
- "This leaves unexplained how citizens evaluate elections they cannot directly observe."
- "The interaction between technical verification and perceived legitimacy has received little systematic attention."

**Weak gap indicators** (avoid):
- "No one has studied X." (Maybe for good reason)
- "More research is needed." (Always true, not a gap)
- "X has been overlooked." (Why does it matter?)

### Step 2c: Question-Raising

Frame the gap as questions.

**Example**:
"This raises a puzzle: if procedural compliance is necessary for legitimacy, why do compliant elections sometimes fail to secure consent? And if compliance is insufficient, what additional conditions must be met?"

### Step 2d: Continuing a Tradition

Position your work as extending prior calls for research.

**Example**:
"Responding to Norris's (2014) call for research on legitimacy mechanisms beyond procedural models, this paper examines..."

**Use when**: A respected scholar has identified the gap you're addressing.

## Move 3: Occupying the Niche

**Goal**: Present your contribution as the answer to the gap.

### Step 3a: Outlining Purpose

State what the paper does.

**Strong purpose statements**:
- "This paper argues that legitimacy in digital voting systems requires not verification per se, but citizen-accessible verification—what we term 'democratic verifiability.'"
- "We develop a framework that integrates procedural and perceptual dimensions of electoral legitimacy."

**Structure**: [This paper] + [action verb] + [specific contribution] + [in relation to gap]

### Step 3b: Announcing Principal Findings

Preview key results.

**Example**:
- "The analysis reveals three mechanisms through which technical opacity undermines democratic legitimacy, even when elections meet legal requirements."

**Caution**: Don't give away everything—create reasons to keep reading.

### Step 3c: Indicating Article Structure

Roadmap the paper.

**Example**:
"The paper proceeds as follows. Section 2 reviews existing legitimacy frameworks. Section 3 develops our concept of democratic verifiability. Section 4 applies this framework to E2E-V voting systems. Section 5 discusses implications for electoral design."

**Function**: Helps readers navigate; signals professional competence.

## CARS Sequence and Cycling

### Basic Sequence
Move 1 → Move 2 → Move 3

### Common Variations

**Multiple cycles**: Large Introductions may repeat the sequence for sub-topics.

**Embedded cycles**: Move 2 might contain mini-Move 1s (establishing territory for specific aspects of the gap).

**Weighted emphasis**: Some papers spend more time on Move 1 (if topic is unfamiliar) or Move 2 (if gap is complex).

## Diagnostic Questions

### Move 1 Failing?
- Is the topic's importance asserted, not just assumed?
- Is prior research organized, not just listed?
- Does the review set up the gap?

### Move 2 Failing?
- Is the gap specific and bounded?
- Is it consequential (does filling it matter)?
- Is it fair to prior work?
- Is it actually a gap (check recent publications)?

### Move 3 Failing?
- Does the contribution directly address the gap?
- Is the contribution specific enough to evaluate?
- Does the roadmap match what the paper actually does?

## Complete Example

**Move 1a (Centrality)**: "How citizens come to accept—or reject—electoral outcomes remains a central question for democratic theory and practice."

**Move 1c (Review)**: "Existing scholarship approaches this question through three lenses: procedural accounts emphasizing rule compliance (Smith, 2015), substantive accounts focusing on outcome quality (Jones, 2018), and perceptual accounts examining citizen attitudes (Brown, 2020)."

**Move 2a (Counter-claim)**: "Yet these approaches share a limiting assumption: that legitimacy is either a property of electoral systems or a perception held by citizens, but not both simultaneously."

**Move 2b (Gap)**: "What remains undertheorized is the mechanism through which system properties translate into citizen perceptions—how technical facts become political trust."

**Move 3a (Purpose)**: "This paper argues that legitimacy requires 'democratic verifiability': not merely that elections are verifiable, but that verification is accessible to ordinary citizens."

**Move 3c (Structure)**: "We develop this argument in four stages..."
